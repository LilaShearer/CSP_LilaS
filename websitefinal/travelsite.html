Interested in visiting Goblin Valley?

Basic Information

If you're interested in a one day trip to Goblin Valley, you can purchase a Day-Use pass here. For those who want to stay longer, there are options available! The Main Campground has a $45 fee that includes a one-vehicle pass. To park an extra vehicle is another $20. If setting up a tent isn't quite your style, you can stay in a Yurt for $150 plus tax. If you're staying in a yurt and want to bring dogs with you, you can have a maximum of two for an extra $20 fee each.


Special Activities

There are other cool activities available at Goblin Valley State Park. Want to get some cool ariel shots of the scenery? A drone pass is $15 per drone. Interested in a wicked rappeling adventure? Purchase a Goblin's Lair Rappel Permit for $4 per person. If you want to buy something to remember your trip by, you can swing by the gift shop in the Visitor Center any time from 9 AM to 5 PM (Please note these times may change depending on the season).


Free Activities

As mentioned above, there are many free services and activities in Goblin Valley State Park. You can enjoy the scenic views, adventure between the rock formations, tear down hills on a mountain bike, and have fun riding nearby OHV trails. Just make sure to bring water!

Important Information

The best times to visit Goblin Valley are in the spring and fall because it is a desert and can become very hot during the summer. It is very dry in the park, so making sure you are hydrated is very important. The park is at 5,000 feet, so those sensitive to lower oxygen levels and not aclimated to the altitude may have an issue. However, this isn't something most people need to worry about.


Fun Facts

Goblin Valley was first discovered by cowboys who were searching for cattle. Arthur Chaffin also stumbeld across the incredible place in 1920 and returned in 1949 to "Mushroom Valley" to photograph the incredible scenery. The state later aquired the site and made it a state park in 1964. The formation in Goblin Valley have an even older history. They were formed over millions of years as softer sandstone was eroded away by water and wind. This left behind the strange figures seen today.
